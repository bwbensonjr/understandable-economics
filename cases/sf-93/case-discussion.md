Great question. The most effective examples for teaching *supply, demand, and willingness to pay* tend to have three traits:

1. People have **very different valuations** for the same thing  
2. Supply is **clearly limited or changes suddenly**  
3. The outcome is **visible and intuitive**, not abstract  

Below are several real-world examples that consistently work well in classrooms and case discussions, with notes on *exactly* which concepts each one illuminates.

---

## 1. Concert Tickets (or Sports Playoff Tickets)

**Why it works:**  
Almost everyone has experienced frustration with ticket prices or sellouts.

### Setup
- A stadium has 20,000 seats (fixed supply).
- Fans have very different willingness to pay:
  - Die-hard fans: $500+
  - Casual fans: $80–150
  - Curious attendees: $30–50

### Supply & Demand
- **Demand curve:** Downward sloping — fewer people want tickets as price rises.
- **Supply curve:** Nearly vertical — number of seats is fixed.

### What people observe in the real world
- Tickets sell out instantly at face value.
- Tickets reappear on resale sites at much higher prices.
- Some seats stay empty despite high resale prices.

### Key lessons
- **Willingness to pay differs across consumers**
- Face value ≠ market-clearing price
- Resellers reveal *true demand*
- Price ceilings (official ticket prices) create shortages

### Teaching moment
Ask:
> “If tickets were priced at $300 initially, who would attend, who wouldn’t, and what problem would disappear?”

---

## 2. Bottled Water After a Natural Disaster

**Why it works:**  
It shows how *the same good* can have radically different value depending on context.

### Setup
- Normal day: bottled water costs $1
- After a hurricane: clean water is scarce

### Demand shift
- Willingness to pay skyrockets because water is now essential.
- Demand curve shifts **rightward** (or upward).

### Supply constraints
- Roads are damaged, deliveries slow.
- Supply curve becomes **steeper** (less responsive).

### What people observe
- Prices rise sharply
- Some stores run out immediately
- Accusations of “price gouging”

### Key lessons
- **Willingness to pay is situational**
- Price increases signal scarcity
- High prices ration limited supply to highest-value uses
- Ethical vs economic perspectives can conflict

### Teaching moment
Ask:
> “If prices were legally frozen at $1, who gets water—and who doesn’t?”

---

## 3. Uber Surge Pricing

**Why it works:**  
It shows supply and demand adjusting *in real time*.

### Setup
- Normal conditions: many drivers, moderate demand
- Rainstorm or big event ends: demand spikes

### What happens
- Price increases automatically
- Some riders drop out (lower willingness to pay)
- More drivers log in (higher supply)

### Visual intuition
- Demand curve shifts right
- Higher price moves market toward equilibrium

### Key lessons
- Prices coordinate behavior without central planning
- Willingness to pay determines who rides now vs later
- Supply responds to incentives

### Teaching moment
Ask:
> “Why does surge pricing reduce wait times?”

---

## 4. Toilet Paper During COVID-19 (Early 2020)

**Why it works:**  
People vividly remember it.

### Setup
- Normal supply chains assume stable demand
- Panic buying causes sudden demand shock

### What people observed
- Empty shelves
- No immediate price increases in many stores
- Hoarding behavior

### Key lessons
- Quantity demanded vs demand
- Short-run supply rigidity
- Non-price rationing (first come, first served)
- Expectations affect demand

### Teaching moment
Ask:
> “Why didn’t factories just ‘make more’ immediately?”

---

## 5. Airline Seats (Economy vs Business Class)

**Why it works:**  
It demonstrates *price discrimination* based on willingness to pay.

### Setup
- Same plane, same destination
- Different prices for seats that cost similar amounts to provide

### Observations
- Business travelers pay more for flexibility
- Leisure travelers accept restrictions for lower prices

### Key lessons
- Firms infer willingness to pay indirectly
- Consumer surplus extraction
- Multiple demand curves for the same product

### Teaching moment
Ask:
> “Why doesn’t the airline just sell all seats at the lowest price?”

---

## One “Perfect” Teaching Case: The Snowstorm Pizza Problem

You can combine all concepts in a single short case:

> A pizza shop has one oven and 50 pizzas. A snowstorm hits.

- Demand increases (people don’t want to cook)
- Supply is fixed in the short run
- Raising prices:
  - Allocates pizzas to those who value them most
  - Encourages drivers to show up
- Keeping prices fixed:
  - Long lines
  - Sold-out signs
  - Angry customers

This case naturally introduces:
- Demand curves
- Willingness to pay
- Equilibrium price
- Rationing mechanisms
- Tradeoffs between fairness and efficiency

## If you want to go one step further

A powerful exercise is to:
1. Give participants $100 in “fake money”
2. Have them secretly write down their max willingness to pay
3. Plot the demand curve from real data
4. Set different prices and observe who buys

Seeing *their own preferences* turn into a curve is often the “aha” moment.

# Teaching Case: Rent Control Expansion in San Francisco

## Background

San Francisco has long had high housing demand driven by:
- Strong job growth (especially tech)
- Geographic constraints
- Desirable amenities and climate

By the early 1990s, rents were rising rapidly, and tenant displacement was a growing political concern.

### The Policy Change (1994)

In 1994, San Francisco expanded its rent control ordinance to include **small multifamily buildings (4 units or fewer)** built before 1980.  

The intent was clear:
- Protect tenants in smaller buildings
- Prevent rapid rent increases
- Preserve “naturally affordable” housing

At the time, roughly **30–40% of the city’s rental housing stock** fell under this new regulation.

---

## The Economic Environment *Before* the Change

### Demand Side
- Many renters with **high willingness to pay** (tech workers, professionals)
- Others with **lower willingness to pay** but strong preference to stay (long-term residents)

This creates a classic downward-sloping demand curve:
- At high rents → fewer renters
- At lower rents → many renters want to live in the city

### Supply Side
- Short run: very inelastic  
  (buildings already exist; construction is slow)
- Long run: potentially elastic *if* new units are allowed and profitable

---

## What Rent Control Does (in Theory)

Rent control imposes a **price ceiling** below the market-clearing rent.

If binding:
- Quantity demanded increases
- Quantity supplied decreases
- A shortage emerges

But housing is durable and heterogeneous, so the real effects unfold over years, not months.

---

## What Happened Next (Unintended Effects)

### 1. Tenants Stayed Longer — Even When It Was Inefficient

Rent-controlled tenants:
- Stayed in units larger than they needed
- Delayed moving for jobs or family changes

**Economic interpretation**
- Rent control increased consumer surplus for incumbent tenants
- But reduced mobility distorted allocation

> Units were not necessarily occupied by those with the highest willingness to pay—or highest need.

---

### 2. Landlords Reduced Rental Supply

Faced with capped rents, many landlords responded rationally:

- Converted rentals to **condos or TICs**
- Redeveloped properties into **single-family homes**
- Removed units from the rental market entirely

**Supply curve effect**
- Long-run supply of rental housing shifted **left**
- Total number of rental units fell

This effect surprised policymakers, who assumed supply would remain roughly fixed.

---

### 3. New Renters Faced Higher Prices

With fewer rental units available:
- Uncontrolled units became scarcer
- Market rents for *new* tenants rose faster than before

So:
- **Incumbent tenants benefited**
- **Future tenants paid more**

This created a two-tier system.

---

### 4. Neighborhood Change Accelerated

Ironically, some protected neighborhoods experienced *faster* gentrification:
- Rental units were removed
- Remaining housing skewed toward ownership
- New supply targeted high-income buyers

---

## Putting the Curves Together

### Before Rent Control
- Market rent balances supply and demand
- Some renters are priced out, but supply incentives remain

### After Rent Control
- Effective price ceiling below equilibrium
- Short run: tenants benefit
- Long run:
  - Supply contracts
  - Demand increases
  - Market rents rise elsewhere

This is a powerful illustration of **short-run vs long-run effects**.

---

## Key Teaching Insights

### Willingness to Pay
- Rent control does **not** allocate housing to highest willingness to pay
- Allocation depends on timing, luck, and tenure

### Supply Matters More Over Time
- Housing supply responses are slow but decisive
- Policies that ignore supply often backfire

### Distributional Effects
- Benefits are concentrated
- Costs are diffuse and delayed
- Political support persists despite aggregate harm

---

## Discussion Questions (Very Effective in Class)

1. Who benefits from rent control? Who pays the cost?
2. Why don’t shortages look like long lines, as in other markets?
3. Why do landlords respond by *removing* units instead of accepting lower profits?
4. How does this policy affect newcomers versus long-time residents?
5. Can you design a policy that protects tenants *without* shrinking supply?

---

- **California statewide rent caps (2019)** with vacancy decontrol
- **Minneapolis zoning reform** (supply-side contrast case)

### Policy Alternatives to Explore
- Housing vouchers
- Upzoning
- Property tax abatements for affordability
- Direct subsidies for new construction

- Real place, real people
- Clear moral intuition → clear economic tension
- Effects unfold over time
- Shows why *intentions ≠ outcomes*

It reliably produces the “wait, that’s not what I expected” moment that makes economic reasoning stick.

# Case A  
## *Holding the Line: Rent Control and the Politics of Urgency in San Francisco (1993)*

### Purpose of the Case  
This case asks readers to step into the role of a city decision maker facing a rapidly worsening housing crisis. It is designed to help policy makers and activists explore how **well-intentioned policies interact with supply, demand, and incentives**, often in ways that are not immediately visible.

The case ends at a **decision point**, before outcomes are known.

---

## The City in Transition

In the early 1990s, San Francisco was changing quickly.

The city had always been desirable, but a new wave of economic activity—technology firms, startups, and professional services—was bringing higher-income workers into neighborhoods that had long been home to working-class families, immigrants, artists, and retirees. Rents were rising faster than wages. Eviction notices were becoming more common. Community meetings that once drew a handful of residents were now packed.

New housing construction was limited. Geography constrained expansion, neighborhood groups opposed large developments, and the permitting process was slow and contentious. Most housing already existed; whatever relief could be offered would have to operate within that reality.

For many residents, the crisis was not abstract. It was personal and immediate.

---

## The Protagonist: Supervisor Elena Martinez

Elena Martinez is a member of the San Francisco Board of Supervisors, representing a district that includes:

- Long-time renters in small apartment buildings  
- Immigrant families living in multi-generational households  
- Small “mom-and-pop” property owners  
- A growing number of young professionals with higher incomes  

Martinez ran for office on a platform of neighborhood stability and social justice. She is not an economist. Her experience is in community organizing and local governance. She believes government has a responsibility to protect residents from being pushed out of their homes by forces beyond their control.

Over the past six months, her office has received a steady stream of calls:

- Tenants whose rents have jumped 20–30 percent in a year  
- Seniors worried they will not be able to remain in their neighborhoods  
- Small landlords concerned about rising costs and shrinking margins  

The pressure is coming from all sides—but especially from tenants, who are organized, vocal, and demanding action.

---

## Existing Rent Control—and Its Limits

San Francisco already has a rent control ordinance. It applies to many multi-unit apartment buildings constructed before 1980 and limits how much rents can increase each year for existing tenants.

However, a large share of the city’s rental housing is **excluded** from these protections, especially:

- Small buildings with four units or fewer  
- In-law units and converted properties  
- Certain owner-occupied rentals  

In Martinez’s district, many of the most vulnerable tenants live in exactly these kinds of buildings.

Tenant advocates argue that this exclusion is arbitrary and unjust.

> “Why should a tenant in a five-unit building have protections, while a tenant next door in a four-unit building has none?”

---

## The Proposal: Expanding Rent Control

A coalition of tenant organizations has drafted a proposal to **expand rent control protections** to include small multifamily buildings (four units or fewer) built before 1980.

The proposal would:
- Cap annual rent increases for covered units  
- Limit landlords’ ability to raise rents for existing tenants  
- Leave vacancy decontrol in place (landlords could reset rent when a tenant leaves)

Supporters frame the proposal as:
- A modest extension of existing policy  
- A way to preserve “naturally affordable” housing  
- An urgent response to displacement

Martinez is being asked to publicly support the measure and help shepherd it through the Board.

---

## The Case for Immediate Action

At a packed evening hearing, tenant after tenant speaks.

One woman describes raising her children in the same apartment for 18 years. Her rent has doubled in five years.

A retired man explains that he cannot afford to move anywhere else in the city.

Advocates argue that:
- Housing is not a luxury good; it is a necessity  
- Markets alone are failing to protect residents  
- Without intervention, entire communities will be lost  

From this perspective, **inaction is itself a policy choice**—one that favors higher-income newcomers over existing residents.

Martinez finds these arguments compelling.

---

## Concerns and Quiet Warnings

Behind closed doors, Martinez hears a different set of concerns.

Some small landlords warn that:
- Their costs (maintenance, taxes, insurance) are rising  
- Rent caps will make some properties financially unsustainable  
- They may be forced to sell, convert, or redevelop  

A city housing analyst raises a more abstract concern:

> “If we reduce the return on rental housing, we should expect fewer rental units over time. The effect won’t be immediate—but it could be real.”

This argument is less emotionally resonant. No one is being displaced *today* by a supply curve shifting years into the future.

Still, Martinez is uneasy. She has seen past policies produce results no one anticipated.

---

## What Is Known—and Unknown

As Martinez weighs her decision, several facts are clear:

- Demand for housing in San Francisco is strong and growing  
- The supply of housing cannot increase quickly  
- Tenants are facing real hardship right now  

What is less clear:

- How many landlords would exit the rental market if rent control expanded  
- Whether reduced rental supply would raise rents elsewhere  
- Who would bear the long-term costs—and whether they would be politically visible  

The proposal offers **certainty of relief today**, but **uncertainty about tomorrow**.

---

## The Decision Point

Supervisor Martinez must decide whether to support the rent control expansion as proposed.

She sees four broad paths forward:

1. **Support the expansion as written**, prioritizing immediate tenant protection  
2. **Support the expansion but insist on complementary measures** to encourage rental supply  
3. **Oppose the expansion** and pursue alternative tools like subsidies or vouchers  
4. **Delay action**, citing the need for further study  

Each choice has consequences—for tenants, landlords, future residents, and the political coalition that brought her to office.

---

## Discussion Questions

1. What problem is Supervisor Martinez trying to solve? How would you define it?
2. Who benefits immediately from rent control expansion? Who might bear costs later?
3. If housing is scarce, what mechanisms determine *who* gets to live in the city?
4. How might different residents’ willingness (or ability) to pay for housing shape outcomes?
5. What assumptions are you making about how landlords and tenants will respond to this policy?
6. If you were in Martinez’s position, what would you do—and why?
